Trace and Analyze Device Traffic

Combine tracert and Wireshark to reveal how your devices route packets to the web

Objective

Use tracert/traceroute and Wireshark to visualize packet flow through routers and boost your Network+ troubleshooting skills.

Step 1: Trace Your Router’s Path

  • Windows: Open Command Prompt, type tracert google.com and press Enter.
  • Linux: Open Terminal, type traceroute google.com. If not installed: sudo apt install traceroute.

Note:

  • The first hop (e.g., 192.168.1.1) is likely your router.
  • Total hops to Google may vary (e.g., 10–15).

Step 2: Capture Router Traffic

  1. Open Wireshark and select your active network interface (e.g., Wi-Fi or Ethernet).
  2. Click the green shark fin icon to start capturing.
  3. In Command Prompt or Terminal, type ping google.com.
  4. Let it ping 10 times, then stop the Wireshark capture (red square icon).

Step 3: Analyze with Wireshark

  • In the filter bar, type icmp and press Enter to isolate ping traffic.
  • Look for echo requests and replies.
  • Note IP addresses: your router (e.g., 192.168.1.1) and Google (e.g., 142.250.190.78).
  • Optional: Cross-check with arp -a (Windows) or ip neigh (Linux) to find MAC addresses.
  • Write down your observations: “Router at 192.168.1.1 forwarded ICMP echo requests to Google.”

Step 4: Reflect

Write a short paragraph answering:

  • How did tracert/traceroute reveal the router’s role?
  • What insights did Wireshark provide?

Hint: tracert shows each hop in the route to a destination. Wireshark reveals how traffic is handled by local and remote devices at the packet level.
